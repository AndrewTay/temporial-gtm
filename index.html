Beachhead strategy · v1 test plan

Sell the rig nobody else can do — to people already paying for it.

Everyone can auto-rig a biped. Our edge is everything that isn't one — quadrupeds, birds, fish, insects, props — plus a good-enough motion loop in minutes. This is the plan for which beachheads to test, what to measure, and the exact signals that say double down or fold.

3 beachheads tested in parallel 8–12 wk to a decision 1 north-star: non-human rig success rate
01 · The wedge

Non-human topology is the beachhead, not the moat

It's our way in — an underserved, jank-tolerant, already-paying pocket of demand the humanoid-rig field can't touch. We win here first because buyers value speed, volume, and emotion over film-grade fidelity. The durable moat is earned on top of this — breadth of topology coverage and the data flywheel from every weird mesh we process — not handed to us by the wedge itself.

Why incumbents leave this open

Mixamo
Humanoid-only. Zero coverage of animals, birds, or objects.
Meshy / Tripo
Generation-first; their auto-rig assumes a biped skeleton and breaks on non-human topology.
Anything World
The one real animal competitor — but library-driven. It maps to pre-built creatures, not your arbitrary mesh.
CSM / Kaedim / Zakeke
Funded gold-rush on generation and static commerce. Not in the rigging-the-weird-mesh lane.

So our entry claim is narrow and sharp

“Send us any non-human mesh — a wolf, a crow, a teapot — and get back a rigged, animated, good-enough asset in minutes, not a week of weight-painting.”

arbitrary topology, not a library good-enough > cinematic speed / volume / emotion buyers global · digital-native · already paying
The one thing to keep proving

Everything below rests on one fact: that we can rig topologically diverse meshes at an accept-quality bar. If that breaks, no channel saves us — see §04.

On objects & ecommerce — mostly a trap

Store 3D is static (turntable + AR placement) and the generation side is a funded gold rush (CSM, Kaedim, Meshy, Zakeke). The only defensible slice is objects that move — furniture folding, gadgets opening, “watch-it-work” demos — via a Shopify app or configurator partnerships. But that skews B2B and slow, so it's a v2 expansion. The better “objects” play is to aim the same articulation at game / AR / tabletop props and reuse the creator channels we're already building.

03 · Beachhead deep-dives

What to ship, measure, and watch — per beachhead

B1

Non-human avatars — VRChat / VTuber / furry

Primary

Sell to commission artists first. They're booked out for months and the slowest, most unloved part of a non-human commission is rigging and weight-painting. Offer to take that off their plate and you're selling a painkiller, not a vitamin. End-creators are the second wave.

Where to find them
BOOTHGumroadJinxxy VGenFurAffinityspecies / avatar Discords VRChat creator X/Twitter
The test offer
  • Rig-as-a-service: “send your model, get a VRC-ready rigged avatar (or clean base rig) back in hours.”
  • 10–20 design-partner artists; route real client work through it.
Primary metrics
  • Rig acceptance rate — output usable with ≤ minor cleanup.
  • WTP per rig — actual paid, vs current commission rates.
  • 30-day repeat rate — artists coming back with new jobs.
Targets to clear
  • ≥ 50% rig acceptance on first pass.
  • ≥ 20% of partners route a second paid job in 30 days.
  • ≥ 1 unprompted referral inside a community.
Signals to double down
  • Artists quietly using it for paid client work (not just the free demo).
  • Organic mentions in species/avatar Discords; inbound from one posted demo.
  • People asking for more species / formats — pull, not push.
Signals to fold
  • They love the demo but won't send paid work.
  • Output needs a full re-rig every time.
  • Topology diversity (wings, tails, digitigrade legs) breaks it repeatedly.
B2

Indie game devs & jammers

Parallel

Win with free tooling and jam presence. Solo and small teams constantly need creatures and props Mixamo can't touch, and jammers are on 48–72h clocks where “rig + idle loop in two minutes” is the difference between shipping and not. Land-grab with a free Unity/Godot plugin; monetize a pro/commercial tier later.

Where to find them
Game Dev Network Discordr/gamedev itch.ioLudum DareGMTK Jam
The test offer
  • Free Unity / Godot plugin: drop in a creature mesh → rig + idle/walk loop.
  • Sponsor or seed one jam; be present in the Discord during it.
Primary metrics
  • Install → activation (first successful non-human rig).
  • Return use across ≥ 2 sessions.
  • Shipped games crediting the plugin.
Targets to clear
  • 200 installs off one jam; ≥ 30% activation.
  • ≥ 15% return after the jam ends.
  • ≥ 5 shipped jam games using it.
Signals to double down
  • Plugin spreads organically between devs.
  • Real feature requests — a sign of genuine use.
  • Games crediting it; Discord pull toward a paid tier.
Signals to fold
  • Installs but no activation.
  • Used only during the free jam, never again.
  • Devs prefer to hand-rig — control beats speed for them.
B3

Tabletop VTT tokens & minis

Parallel

The easiest quality bar we'll face. Tokens are ~100% non-human and the animation need is a single 4-second idle loop. There's a proven Patreon market (Beneos, Happy Tokenz). Sell to creators who resell — or run our own Patreon — built on batch throughput.

Where to find them
Foundry VTT DiscordRoll20 communities r/FoundryVTTCults3Dtoken Patreons
The test offer
  • Batch pipeline: static token → animated idle loop at volume.
  • Pilot with 3–5 existing token creators on a rev-share or flat fee.
Primary metrics
  • Cost / time per animated token vs hand-made.
  • Throughput at the quality bar (tokens/day).
  • Creator adoption & end-GM download pull.
Targets to clear
  • ≥ 10× faster than hand-made at acceptable quality.
  • ≥ 2 creators move a real pack through the pipeline.
  • Early Patreon MRR or a paid pack.
Signals to double down
  • A creator switches their pipeline to us.
  • GMs request more sets; downloads climb.
  • Patreon MRR compounds month over month.
Signals to fold
  • Quality bar fails at small scale (tokens are tiny but loops still read).
  • Market too small or too cheap to monetize.
  • Creators won't share margin and won't pay a fee.

v2 · secondary

04 · Measurement framework

One north-star, one funnel, every channel

North-star · product truth

Non-human Rig Success Rate

The share of arbitrary, topologically diverse non-human meshes that produce an accept-quality rig + loop with ≤ minor cleanup. This is the wedge quantified — and the coverage that compounds into a real moat. Track it per topology class (quadruped, bird, fish, insect, prop) so you see exactly where coverage is thin. If this number is low, channel metrics are noise — fix the engine before scaling spend.

Under the north-star, every channel runs the same funnel. Watch for the drop-off stage — that's what to fix or fold on.

Demand-intensity proxies (look before you build)

  • › Commission backlog length — how booked-out are non-human artists?
  • › Frequency of “can anyone rig my [animal]?” asks in communities.
  • › Price of comparable commissions — anchors our pricing.
  • › Existing paid Patreons in the niche (proves money flows).

The metrics that actually decide it

  • › Activation rate — the single best leading indicator of value.
  • › Paid conversion & repeat — revealed willingness to pay.
  • › Referral coefficient — these communities are small & high-trust: virality or death.
  • › CAC via community — should be ≈ free; if you're buying users, the wedge is wrong.
05 · The test

An 8-week run to a real decision

Tight, instrumented, and biased toward putting real paid work through the engine — not demos. Pricing is part of the experiment, not an afterthought.

Week 0

Instrument & recruit

Define the accept-quality bar with examples. Stand up event tracking for the funnel. Line up 10–20 design partners per beachhead from the channels in §03.

Weeks 1–2

Ship the minimal offer

B1: rig-as-a-service intake. B2: plugin alpha into one jam. B3: batch pipeline for 3–5 token creators. Each should let a partner get one real asset out.

Weeks 3–6

Run real work through it

Push paid client jobs (B1), jam projects (B2), and token packs (B3). Capture rig-success-rate by topology, activation, repeat, and actual payment. Test 2–3 price points.

Weeks 7–8

Decision gate

Score each beachhead against the gate below. Concentrate on one; keep at most one parallel; kill the rest cleanly.

Pricing experiments to run

  • $ Per-rig / per-asset (B1, B3) — anchor against existing commission prices.
  • $ Free tool + paid commercial/pro tier (B2) — land free, monetize the studio.
  • $ Creator rev-share or our own Patreon (B3).
  • $ Test the painkiller framing (“skip the weight-painting”) vs volume framing.

Guardrails for a clean test

  • ! Measure paid behavior, not stated intent.
  • ! Don't over-serve design partners by hand — it hides the real activation rate.
  • ! Keep CAC honest: community pull should be near-free, not bought.
  • ! Give the engine 2 iterations on a quality gap before folding a beachhead.
06 · The decision

Scale, iterate, or fold

At week 8, every beachhead lands in exactly one column. Be disciplined: a beloved demo that won't convert is a fold, not an iterate.

Double down if…
  • Rig success ≥ bar across the topology classes this audience uses.
  • Multiple paid repeats — same users coming back with money.
  • Organic referral present inside the community.
  • CAC ≈ free — growth is pull, not push.
Iterate if…
  • Strong love & activation but weak monetization → fix pricing/packaging.
  • Strong demand but quality gaps → one more engine iteration.
  • It's working but the wrong buyer (end-creator not artist, GM not creator).
Fold if…
  • No activation — they won't get a first asset out.
  • No willingness to pay after price tests.
  • Quality can't clear the bar after 2 iterations.
  • Market is real but too small or too cheap to matter.
Default expectation

The base case is that B1 (non-human avatars) is the wedge — densest demand, jank-tolerant, clearest painkiller. Run B3 (tokens) in parallel as the easiest quality win and earliest revenue, and treat B2 (game devs) as the top-of-funnel land-grab. Hold the animated pet twin as the consumer breakout for when the engine and channels are proven.

07 · Geography & focus

Singapore is no disadvantage

These communities are global and digital-native, payment rails work from SG, and a no-name student team starts with a cost edge and a timezone advantage over the biggest avatar market on earth.

Lean in

  • Japan timezone overlap — the largest avatar/VTuber market, in your working hours.
  • Cost edge — a small team can undercut booked-out Western commission artists on the unloved rigging step.
  • Local design partners — IMDA PIXEL, Gamescom Asia, and NUS / NTU / SMU game-dev clubs.
  • Global rails — Gumroad, BOOTH, Patreon, itch.io all pay out to SG.

Avoid

  • Film & AAA — cinematic polish is exactly what we don't have.
  • Scientific / medical viz — accuracy bar kills “good-enough.”
  • Enterprise sales — slow cycles a student team can't fund.
  • Static ecommerce 3D — doesn't touch our rigging edge; crowded & funded.
